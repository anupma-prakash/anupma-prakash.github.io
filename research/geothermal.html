Geothermal Exploration and Assessment

Geothermal resources are associated with several subtle surface manifestations, or geo-environmental indicators. Mapping these surface indicators using remote sensing techniques, can indirectly lead to the identification and assessment of the resource.

The most obvious surface indicator is an anomaly in surface temperatures associated with the buried heat source. Imaging in the thermal infrared (TIR) region is a very useful tool for mapping such temperature anomalies. Depending on the size, depth, and nature of the buried hot source, one can see the surface signature on airborne images, and on satellite images. We routinely process thermal images from the Landsat Thematic Mapper and ASTER satellite sensors to identify warm areas on the land surface, and follow-up with an airborne TIR survey (e.g. using a broad band FLIR sensor), to narrow down the anomaly area. The images not only provide a synoptic overview of the land surface temperature, but can also be processed to provide quantitative estimates of surface kinetic temperature, depth of buried source, thermal flux, and a first order estimate of the geothermal potential of the resource.

Other geoenvironmental indicators associated with a geothermal area that are amenable to detection with remote sensing include, but are not limited to, conductive crustal fault structures, thermal springs, sinters, evaporites, hydrothermally altered mineral zones, stressed vegetation. Besides exploration and assessment, remote sensing derived information in conjunction with other information integrated in a geographic information system (GIS) can assist is decision making for thermal plant site selection and site management.
